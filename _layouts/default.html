Emma Dickson



Info

Emma Dickson is fascinated by outdated technology and the process of translation and obsolescence in technical languages. They both produce and work as a technician on time-based media and digital art. Their net art and new media sculptures explore identity, community, and longing through tech. In Winter 2019, they were the Toolmaker-in-Residence at Signal Cultures in Owego, NY, where they began to develop a new, multimodal form of slow scan TV (SSTV). Emma currently lives in Durham North Carolina, where they skate with Bull City Roller Derby and are employed as a Data Engineer with Artsy and as a General Developer for Webrecorder.

    Dead Languages

    Dead Languages is a work in progress.

    Dead languages explores the data loss inherent in old media and how that reflects our experience of memory.

    It’s an exploration of the enormous rift between our own narration of our experiences and other’s perceptions of our life. Using found slides it mirrors the reality of trying to grapple with any complex idea, we are limited by the tools and experiences we’ve accumulated.

    I'm Beside Myself

    “Beside Myself” is an interactive installation, it acts essentially as a personal dissociation simulator. Users slip on a VR headset equipped with miniature lcds and voluntarily enter a space of anxiety and removal from themselves. A camera dangles above their head and shows them a view of the space in front of them higher and more downward cast than their head. This forced removal from their own perspective invites them to consider the ways in which they are always engaging in self alienation and reconciliation.

    “Beside Myself” is interested in the self consciousness around how we relate to others and the world around us. How we make peace with the inability to perfectly communicate ourselves to others.

    Closed Circuit

    Closed Circuit is an ongoing series of sculptures created from personal tv/radio’s.

    Each piece in the series approaches the crt television as an individual with it’s own unique character. The television is disassembled, and reconstructed with custom alterations to its circuitry. A security camera is incorporated into the tv’s antenna and connected to a version of Tetsuo Kogawa's micro tv transmitter. The television is then tuned in to receive the transmission of itself. This results in a sculpture that is forced to view and display itself. Each configuration of a television in this series is unique.

    Closed Circuits explores introspection and the gap between how we perceive ourselves and how we appear to others. By forcing a technical object to undergo introspection it encourages the audience to identify with the machine in it’s imposed self scrutiny. It reverses the assumed metaphor for electronic pieces, that electronics represent an evolution away from humanity and instead suggests that they can be used to understand human nature.
